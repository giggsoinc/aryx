aryx.example.com/start
A R Y X

Aryx connects your data from a business thinking view.

A discovery-driven knowledge graph built on auto-discovered ontology frameworks. The structure emerges from your data and your goals, instead of being designed in advance.

How it works
  1. 1
    You tell Aryx what you want to figure out
    2–5 goals or questions. That's the brief.
  2. 2
    You point Aryx at your data
    Database, files, APIs — whatever you have.
  3. 3
    Aryx proposes the model, you approve
    Records get deduped, things get linked into a map. You stay in the loop on anything ambiguous.
  4. 4
    You ask, Aryx answers
    Plain English questions. Every answer cites the records behind it.

Takes about 3 minutes.

Two readings, same screen. The headline is for everyone. The italicised line names what Aryx is in the ontology world — without jargon. Four steps explain the contract: you bring goals + data, Aryx proposes structure, you approve, you query.

Goals are concrete. "What your business does" is the Pacific Ocean. Start where the user actually has an answer.

What do you want Aryx to help you figure out?

List 2–5 questions or goals. Plain English. The nouns become your data model automatically.

📎 Or drop a doc / SOW / strategy deck Skip
Or pick a starter to adapt:
Find customers at risk of churning Predict which deals will close this quarter Spot patients overdue for follow-up See where my BOM has single-source risk

Why goals first, not domain: "What does your business do?" is unanswerable — people ramble for paragraphs. "What do you want to figure out?" is concrete; even a doer can list 2–3 questions in 30 seconds. Same payload to brief_draft, but the AI gets aim + objectives first and derives the domain. METHONTOLOGY-aligned: aim drives everything else.

verbs in goals → aim each goal line → objective nouns in goals → classes / entity types questions in goals → competency questions (the test of "did the model work?")

Under the covers · What Aryx does with your data
Shown once after the user clicks Connect — sets expectations. Same diagram repeats on Screen 5 with live progress.
aryx.example.com/start?step=pipeline

Here's what happens next.

📥
Read
Stream records from every source you picked — one at a time.
e.g. 1,200 customer rows + 47 PDFs
›
🏷
Tag fields
Recognise what each field means — "looks like an email", "looks like a date".
e.g. cust_em → email_address
›
🧩
Dedupe
Find the same record across sources, even when spelled differently.
e.g. "Acme Corp" + "Acme Corporation" → one
›
🔗
Link
Figure out how things relate — tickets to customers, devices to tickets.
e.g. T-1043 opened_by Acme
›
🗺
Map
Build the queryable graph and the canvas you'll review.
e.g. 4 record kinds, 1,847 records, 47k connections
›
💬
Ready
Ask anything in plain English. Aryx cites the records behind every answer.
e.g. "Which agents resolve network tickets fastest?"
You stay in control. Whenever Aryx isn't sure — "Is Customer the same as Account?" — it pauses and asks. You answer, it continues.

Why this earns its place: people only trust automation they can see. This one diagram is the only place Aryx explains its pipeline to a user — on the first run, between picking sources and seeing data flow. After that, Screen 5 reuses the same shape with live progress on each step. No second engineering doc, no surprises.

Step 4 · Connect (if Database)
Four fields tops. Plain-English labels. Password line says "encrypted".
aryx.example.com/start

Connect to your database

Your database server.
🔒 Encrypted on save · never shown again
Advanced (port, dialect, options)
What happens when you click Connect
Aryx opens a test connection, lists your tables (read-only), and shows you what it sees. Your password is encrypted before it touches disk with a key only the server holds; the UI never shows it again.

"Advanced" hides: port, dialect dropdown, extra-context textarea, schema filter. Default is Postgres on 5432. Most users never expand it.
